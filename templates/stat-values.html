Statistical Values:

For the selected column, we compute a comprehensive set of descriptive statistics to summarize the data. The sample size represents the total number of observations in the dataset. The sample mean provides the average value, while the sample variance and sample standard deviation measure the variability and spread of the data around the mean. In addition, we report the minimum and maximum values to indicate the data range. Quartile statistics—including the first quartile (Q1), median (Q2), and third quartile (Q3)—are calculated to describe the distribution and central tendency of the data, helping identify skewness, spread, and potential outliers. These summary measures provide a concise numerical overview of the dataset’s key characteristics.

  • Sample size (n)
  • Sample mean, \(\bar{x} = \displaystyle\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_{i}\)
  • Sample variance, \(s^2 = \displaystyle \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i}-\bar{x})^{2} = \displaystyle \frac{n\sum_{i=1}x_i^2 - (\sum_{i=1}x_i)^2}{n(n-1)}\)
  • Sample standard deviation, \(s = \displaystyle\sqrt{\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i}-\bar{x})^{2}}\)
  • Minimum value
  • 25% percentile (Q1)
  • 50% percentile (Median, Q2)
  • 75% percentile (Q3)
  • Maximum value


Box-plot:

A box plot is a concise graphical tool used to summarize the distribution of a dataset by highlighting its central tendency, variability, and overall spread. It is based on five key statistics: the minimum value, first quartile (Q1), median, third quartile (Q3), and maximum value. The box itself represents the interquartile range (IQR), which contains the middle 50% of the data, while the line inside the box indicates the median. Lines known as “whiskers” extend from the box to show the range of the data excluding extreme values, and points plotted beyond the whiskers represent potential outliers. Box plots are especially useful for comparing multiple datasets side by side, assessing skewness, detecting unusual observations, and gaining quick insights into data variability



Normal Probability Plot:

A normal probability plot, also known as a Q–Q plot, is a graphical method used to evaluate whether a dataset can be reasonably modeled by a normal distribution. This is done by comparing the ordered sample observations with the corresponding theoretical quantiles from a standard normal distribution. If the data are approximately normally distributed, the plotted points will align closely along a straight line; systematic departures from linearity indicate skewness, heavy tails, or the presence of outliers.

To construct the plot, the sample data are first arranged in increasing order, denoted as \(x_{(1)}, x_{(2)}, \dots, x_{(n)}\) Each ordered observation \(x_{(i)}\) is then paired with a theoretical normal quantile \(z_i\), forming points of the form \(\left(z_i, x_{(i)} \right)\) for \(i = 1, 2, \dots, n\). The theoretical quantiles are calculated using \[z_i = \phi^{-1}\left(\frac{i - 0.5}{n}\right)\] where \(\phi^{-1}\) denotes the inverse cdf of the standard normal distribution. By visually comparing the plotted points to a reference line, the normal probability plot provides an intuitive and effective way to assess normality and identify deviations from the assumed distribution.



Histogram:

The shape of a histogram provides valuable insight into the distributional characteristics of a dataset. For instance, a bell-shaped histogram often suggests that the data may approximately follow a normal distribution, while a right-skewed histogram indicates a concentration of smaller values with a longer tail extending toward larger values. Similarly, a left-skewed histogram reflects many larger values and relatively few smaller ones. The presence of multiple peaks may indicate multimodality or the existence of subgroups within the data. In addition, the choice of bin width plays a crucial role in interpretation, as bins that are too wide can obscure important features, whereas bins that are too narrow may introduce unnecessary noise.
