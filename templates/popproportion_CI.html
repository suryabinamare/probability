Point Estimation and Confidence Intervals for a Population Proportion

Suppose we have a sample of size \(n\) with \(x\) successes (or have a specified attribute). The sample proportion, \(\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}\) is the ratio of successes in the sample. In orther words, a sample proportion is the ratio of values that hold a specified attribute divided by the total sample size. The sample proportion, \(\hat{p}\), is a random variable that estimates the population proportion, \(p\).

The distribution of \(\hat{p}\) is approximately normal if the sample size is large enough, with mean \(\mu_{\hat{p}} = p\) and standard deviation \(\sigma_{\hat{p}} = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\). As \(p\) is unknown, we cannot use this distribution. Instead we replace \(p\) with \(\hat{p}\). And for large sample size and \(hat{p}\) such that both \(n.\bar{p}\) and \(n.(1-\hat{p})\) is greater than 5. We use the z-distribution for \(\hat{p}\)

Point Estimate and Confidence Interval:

  1. Calculate the sample proportion \(\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}\), the unbiased point estimate of the population proportion.
  2. Determine the critical value (\(z_{\text{crit}} = z_{\alpha/2}\)):
  3. Compute the standard error, \(se = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\).
  4. Margin of error, \(me = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot se\)
  5. (\(1-\alpha\))% confidence interval:
    CI = \( \displaystyle \hat{p} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} \)
    or
    CI = \( \left(\hat{p} - z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}, \hat{p} + z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\right)\).
    or
    CI = \(\qquad \left(\hat{p} - me, \hat{p} + me\right)\)

Hypothesis Testing for a Proportion:

We perform hypothesis testing for a population proportion. We use the z-test to determine whether to reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\), based on the sample data. The steps involved in hypothesis testing are as follows:

  1. Two-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad p = p_0\)        
    • \(H_a : \quad p \neq p_0\)        
  2. Left-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad p = p_0\)        
    • \(H_a : \quad p < p_0\)        
  3. Right-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad p = p_0\)        
    • \(H_a : \quad p > p_0\)        

Provide the following values:

  • Sample size: \(n\)
  • Sample Proportion: \(\hat{p}\)
  • Population Proportion: \(p_0\)
  • Significance Level: \(\alpha\)

We find the following values:

  • Test Statistic, \(z_{\text{test}}\) = \(\displaystyle \frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}}\)
  • Critical Value: \(z_{\text{crit}}\)
  • P-Value:
    • \(= P(z < |z_{\text{test}}|) \) = area to the left and right of \(z_{\text{test}}\) (two-tailed test)
    • \(= P(z > z_{\text{test}}) \) = area to the right of \(z_{\text{test}}\) (right-tailed test)
    • \(= P(z < z_{\text{test}}) \) = area to the left of \(z_{\text{test}}\) (left-tailed test)

Conclusion:

  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if \(z_{\text{test}}\) is in the non-rejection region. Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
    • OR
  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if P-Value is bigger than \(\alpha\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
