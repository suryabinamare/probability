Parameter Estimation and Hypothesis Testing: A Population Mean (known variance)

We focus on estimating and testing hypotheses about a population mean, \(\mu\), when the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is known.

  1. Parameter Estimation: The sample mean is used to estimate the population mean. It is an unbiased estimator of \(\mu\) with a standard error of \( \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \), where \(n\) is the sample size. Sometimes finding a range of values is more important than a single point estimate. This is done by constructing a confidence interval in which the parameter lies.
  2. Hypothesis Testing: We test hypotheses about the population mean using the Z-test when \(\sigma\) is known. The null hypothesis, \(H_0\), typically states that the population mean is equal to a specific value, \(\mu_0\). The alternative hypothesis, \(H_a\), can be two-tailed or one-tailed, depending on the research question.

Confidence Interval:

Using sample data and population standard deviaiton, we construct (1-\(\alpha\))100% confidence interval for the population mean, \(\mu\). If the significance level, \(\alpha\) is 0.05, then we are (1-0.05)100% = 95% confident that the interval contains the true population mean, \(\mu\). We interpret this as if we were to take many samples and construct confidence intervals for each sample, approximately 95% of those intervals would contain the true population mean, \(\mu\).

To construct the confidence interval, we use the following steps:

1. Determine the sample mean (\(\bar{x}\)), population standard deviation (\(\sigma\)), sample size (\(n\)), and significance level (\(\alpha\)).
2. Find the critical Z-value (\(z_{\alpha/2}\)) from the standard normal distribution table corresponding to the desired confidence level.
3. Calculate the margin of error (ME) using the formula:
\[ ME = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]
4. Construct the confidence interval (CI) using the formula:
\[P(-z_{\alpha/2} \leq Z \leq z_{\alpha/2}) = 1 - \alpha \] \[P(-z_{\alpha/2} \leq \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} \leq z_{\alpha/2}) = 1 - \alpha \] We solve the inequality for \(\mu\) to get the confidence interval: \[ -z_{\alpha/2} \leq \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} \leq z_{\alpha/2}\] \[ \bar{x} - z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \leq \mu \leq \bar{x} + z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \] Thus, the (1-\(\alpha\))100% confidence interval is given by: \[ CI = \left(\bar{x} - \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha/2}, \quad \bar{x} + \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha/2}\right) \] or \[ CI = \left(\bar{x} - ME, \quad \bar{x} + ME\right) \]

Margin of Error and Sample size:

The margin of error (ME) represents the maximum expected difference between the sample mean and the true population mean within a specified confidence level. It quantifies the uncertainty associated with estimating the population mean from a sample. A smaller margin of error indicates a more precise estimate of the population mean. The margin of error is influenced by factors such as the sample size, population standard deviation, and the desired confidence level.

To calculate the required sample size (n) for estimating a population mean with a specified margin of error (ME) and confidence level, we use the formula: \[-z_{\alpha/2} \leq \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} \leq z_{\alpha/2}\] and \[-z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \leq \bar{x} - \mu \leq z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\] \[ |\bar{x}-\mu| \leq z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \] So the difference between sample mean and population mean is called Margin of Error (ME). For the \[(1-\alpha)100%\] confidence interval, the margin of error is given by: \[ ME = z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \] Rearranging the formula to solve for sample size (n), we get: \[ n = \left( \frac{z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sigma}{ME} \right)^2 \]

This formula can be used to determine the minimum sample size required to achieve a desired margin of error for a given confidence level.

Hypothesis Testing for Population Mean (Ïƒ known)

We perform hypothesis testing for a population mean when the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is known. We use the Z-test to determine whether to reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\), based on the sample data.

  1. Two-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu \neq \mu_0\)
  2. Left-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu < \mu_0\)
  3. Right-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu > \mu_0\)

The steps involved in hypothesis testing are as follows:

Provide the following values:
  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x}\)
  • Population mean: \(\mu_0\)
  • Sample size: \(n\)
  • Pop Std: \(\sigma\)
  • Significance Level: \(\alpha\)

We find the following values:

  • Test Statistic, \(Z\) = \( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \)
  • P-value:
    • \( P(z > |Z|) \) for two-tailed test (area under the curve in both tails beyond \(\pm |Z|\))
    • \( P(Z < z) \) for right-tailed test (area under the curve to the right of \(Z\))
    • \( P(Z > z) \) for left-tailed test (area under the curve to the left of \(Z\))
  • Critical Value:
    • \(\pm z_{\alpha/2}\) (for two-tailed test)
    • \( z_{\alpha} \) (for right-tailed test)
    • -\( z_{\alpha} \) (for left-tailed test)
  • Non - rejection Region for \(\bar{x}\):
    • \(\left(\mu_0 - \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha/2}, \quad \mu_0 + \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha/2}\right)\) for two-tailed test.
    • \( \left( -\infty, \quad \mu_0 + \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha} \right) \) for right-tailed test.
    • \( \left(\mu_0 - \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}z_{\alpha}, \quad \infty \right) \) for left-tailed test.
  • Non - rejection Region for \(Z\):
    • \(\left(-z_{\alpha/2}, \quad z_{\alpha/2}\right)\) for two-tailed test.
    • \( \left( -\infty, \quad z_{\alpha} \right) \) for right-tailed test.
    • \( \left(-z_{\alpha}, \quad \infty \right) \) for left-tailed test.

Conclusion:

  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if Test Statistic, \(Z\) is in the non-rejection interval for \(Z\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
    • OR
  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if P-Value is bigger than \(\alpha\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
    • OR
  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if the sample mean (\(\bar{x}\)) is in the non-rejection interval for \(\bar{x}\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
