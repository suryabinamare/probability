Parameter Estimation and Hypothesis Testing: A Population Mean (unknown variance)

We focus on estimating and testing hypotheses about a population mean, \(\mu\), when the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is unknown.

  1. Parameter Estimation: The sample mean is used to estimate the population mean. It is an unbiased estimator of \(\mu\) with a standard error of \( \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \), where \(n\) is the sample size. Sometimes finding a range of values is more important than a single point estimate. This is done by constructing a confidence interval in which the parameter lies.
  2. Hypothesis Testing: We test hypotheses about the population mean using the T-test when \(\sigma\) is unknown. The null hypothesis, \(H_0\), typically states that the population mean is equal to a specific value, \(\mu_0\). The alternative hypothesis, \(H_a\), can be two-tailed or one-tailed, depending on the research question.

Confidence Interval:

Using sample data and t-distribution, we construct (1-\(\alpha\))100% confidence interval for the population mean, \(\mu\). If the significance level, \(\alpha\) is 0.05, then we are (1-0.05)100% = 95% confident that the interval contains the true population mean, \(\mu\). We interpret this as if we were to take many samples and construct confidence intervals for each sample, approximately 95% of those intervals would contain the true population mean, \(\mu\). We use the t-distribution because the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is unknown and we estimate it using the sample standard deviation, \(s\).

To construct the confidence interval, we use the following steps:

1. Determine the sample mean (\(\bar{x}\)), sample standard deviation (\(s\)), sample size (\(n\)), and significance level (\(\alpha\)).
2. Find the critical t-value (\(t_{\alpha/2, \, df}\)) from the t-distribution table corresponding to the desired confidence level and degrees of freedom (\(df = n - 1\)).
3. Calculate the margin of error (ME) using the formula:
\[ ME = t_{\alpha/2, \, df} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
4. Construct the confidence interval (CI) using the formula:
\[P(-t_{\alpha/2, \, df} \leq T \leq t_{\alpha/2, \, df}) = 1 - \alpha \] \[P(-t_{\alpha/2, \, df} \leq \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}} \leq t_{\alpha/2, \, df}) = 1 - \alpha \] We solve the inequality for \(\mu\) to get the confidence interval: \[ -t_{\alpha/2, \, df} \leq \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}} \leq t_{\alpha/2, \, df}\] \[ \bar{x} - t_{\alpha/2, \, df} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \leq \mu \leq \bar{x} + t_{\alpha/2, \, df} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \] Thus, the (1-\(\alpha\))100% confidence interval is given by: \[ CI = \left(\bar{x} - \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha/2, \, df}, \quad \bar{x} + \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha/2, \, df}\right) \] or \[ CI = \left(\bar{x} - ME, \quad \bar{x} + ME\right) \]

Hypothesis Testing for Population Mean (Ïƒ unknown)

We perform hypothesis testing for a population mean when the population standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is unknown. We use the t-test to determine whether to reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis, \(H_0\), based on the sample data. The steps involved in hypothesis testing are as follows:

  1. Two-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu \neq \mu_0\)
  2. Left-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu < \mu_0\)
  3. Right-tailed Test:
    • \(H_0 : \quad \mu = \mu_0\)
    • \(H_a : \quad \mu > \mu_0\)

Provide the following values:

  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x}\)
  • Population mean: \(\mu_0\)
  • Sample size: \(n\)
  • Sample Std: \(s\)
  • Significance Level: \(\alpha\)

We find the following values:

  • Test Statistic, \(T\) = \( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}} \)
  • P-value:
    • \( P(t > |T|) \) for two-tailed test (area under the curve in both tails beyond \(\pm |T|\))
    • \( P(T < t) \) for right-tailed test (area under the curve to the right of \(T\))
    • \( P(T > t) \) for left-tailed test (area under the curve to the left of \(T\))
  • Critical Value:
    • \(\pm t_{\alpha/2}\) (for two-tailed test)
    • \( t_{\alpha} \) (for right-tailed test)
    • -\( t_{\alpha} \) (for left-tailed test)
  • Non - rejection Region for \(\bar{x}\):
    • \(\left(\mu_0 - \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha/2}, \quad \mu_0 + \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha/2}\right)\) for two-tailed test.
    • \( \left( -\infty, \quad \mu_0 + \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha} \right) \) for right-tailed test.
    • \( \left(\mu_0 - \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}t_{\alpha}, \quad \infty \right) \) for left-tailed test.
  • Non - rejection Region for \(T\):
    • \(\left(-t_{\alpha/2}, \quad t_{\alpha/2}\right)\) for two-tailed test.
    • \( \left( -\infty, \quad t_{\alpha} \right) \) for right-tailed test.
    • \( \left(-t_{\alpha}, \quad \infty \right) \) for left-tailed test.

Conclusion:

  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if Test Statistic, \(T\) is in the non-rejection interval for \(T\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
    • OR
  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if P-Value is bigger than \(\alpha\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
    • OR
  • Do not reject \(H_0\) if the sample mean (\(\bar{x}\)) is in the non-rejection interval for \(\bar{x}\). Else reject \(H_0\) in favor of alternative hypothesis.
